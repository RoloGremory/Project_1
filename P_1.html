How to build your own PC



Building your own PC may seem like a daunting project, especially for a Rookie. You might be

worried it’s too complex, too expensive, or too time-consuming, but no worries now!


In this guide, we’ll explain how to build a PC step by step, starting with understanding your PC needs,

exploring the different parts of a computer, and guiding you through the PC build process.



Read through our guide for easy-to-follow instructions or view our installation videos for visual demonstrations.

    Highlights:


  • Building your own PC is the best way to ensure that your machine will meet all of your needs and preferences.

  • Consider what kind of case you want before choosing your components, as well as your budget.

  • Start with the CPU when planning your build, as your choice will determine your system’s level of performance and compatibility with next-gen components.

  • Building a PC using separate, easy-to-follow steps is a great way to learn about how each individual component works.

What Kind of PC Do You Want to Build?

As with any project, knowing your goal is the best place to begin.

Are you a gamer seeking a high-performance gaming rig, a student

needing a versatile research and editing machine, or someone who

wants a reliable computer for everyday tasks?


Once you’ve identified your purpose, you can determine the hardware

and performance required, ensuring you only pay for what you truly need.





What’s Your Budget?

The cost of building a PC varies widely depending on your goals. Setting

a realistic budget beforehand is essential.

High-performance components, such as faster processors or newer

memory technology, tend to cost more. However, building your own

PC gives you the flexibility to prioritize certain components and save

money where it’s less critical.

Memory (RAM) and storage are often significant expenses, but purchasing

only what you need upfront can be more cost-effective than upgrading pre

-built systems.





Essential Hardware Components

There are five primary hardware categories you’ll need to research:

  1. MotherBoard
  2. Processor (CPU)
  3. Memory (RAM)
  4. Storage (SSD / HDD)
  5. Case, Fans, and Power Supply (PSU)


Additional items like the operating system, monitor, keyboard, and

mouse are also necessary but won’t significantly affect performance.


  1. MotherBoard

    2. The motherboard connects all the PC’s components, making it a

    critical starting point. It determines compatibility with other parts,

    such as the processor, RAM type, and storage options.

    Ensure your motherboard supports the hardware you plan to use,

    considering factors like processor power, memory technology

    (DDR4, DDR5, etc.), and storage interface (SATA or PCIe).



  2. Processor (CPU)

    3. The CPU is your PC’s powerhouse, dictating overall performance.

    Faster CPUs with higher GHz ratings perform better but may

    require additional cooling due to higher energy consumption.

    Choose a CPU that balances your performance needs and budget,

    keeping cooling solutions in mind.



  3. Memory (RAM)

    4. RAM improves system performance by temporarily storing active

    data. It’s crucial for multitasking and running resource-intensive

    applications.

    Check your motherboard’s specifications for supported RAM types

    and capacity to avoid purchasing incompatible or unnecessary

    modules.



  4. Storage (SSD / HDD)

    5. Modern PCs benefit greatly from SSDs over traditional HDDs due to

    their speed and energy efficiency. Choose the storage form factor

    and capacity that suits your needs.



  5. Case, Fans, and Power Supply (PSU)

    6. Your PC case, cooling system, and power supply should align with your

    build’s requirements. High-performance PCs need adequate airflow and

    a reliable power supply to avoid overheating and ensure stable operation.


    Pro Tip: Use zip ties to tidy cables and improve airflow inside your case.
